# Sample deep-research memo (prototype 04, W9 stage 1)

**Question (run live, in-session, driving the F1 corpus via `research_tool.py`):**
*How has the treatment of "special-skills / competency" skipping under Education
Code §44955(d)(1) evolved from the 1980s to the 2010s — has the bar for
justifying a skip risen, and what showing succeeds vs. fails?*

Every citation below was retrieved and read from the corpus during the session;
cites are **District (ALJ)** only, per the volumes' convention.

## Answer in one paragraph

The *substantive* standard has been stable since the early 1980s: a district may
"skip" a junior employee only by proving (1) a specific need for a specific
course or program, (2) that the junior possesses special training and experience
*necessary* for it, and (3) that the more-senior employees do **not** possess
that training — the rule traceable to *Alexander* (1983) and *Poppers* (1986).
The bar did not rise. What changed is **form and failure-locus**: by 2010–2012
ALJs recite an explicit multi-prong test almost verbatim, and the districts'
recurring losses migrated from "is this teacher competent?" to two drafting/proof
failures — (a) invoking a competency or skip criterion that was never written
into the Board's PKS resolution, and (b) proving the junior's skill while failing
to prove the senior teachers lack it. The "we kept the better candidate"
rationale is a stable loser across all eras.

## The trajectory

**Early era (1981–1983): the standard is set, litigated case-by-case.**
- A district could skip coordinators based on "background, training and practical
  experience, and the recency thereof" where senior respondents had *some*
  experience but not the training/recency of those retained — Tamalpais
  (Kendall), 1981.
- But where a board **did not establish a competency standard** and made no
  determination that senior teachers were not competent and credentialed, the
  juniors **could not be skipped** — Pleasanton (Coan), 1983. "Legitimate
  concerns" about a senior teacher's competency were "not sufficient to establish
  [in]competency" — Livermore Valley (Sarkisian), 1983.
- Districts got deference on genuinely novel/experimental subjects absent bad
  faith or abuse of discretion — El Segundo (Gruen), 1981.

**Modern era (2008–2012): the same rule, now a recited multi-prong test.**
- The test is stated almost identically across districts: (1) a specific need for
  a specific course/study, (2) senior teachers do not possess the special
  training/experience necessary, (3) the junior does — Fontana (Walker), 2011 &
  2012; Temecula Valley (Walker), 2011 & 2012; Beverly Hills (Ruiz), 2010.
- The anchor authority is explicit: *Alexander* (1983) 139 Cal.App.3d 567 —
  Gilroy (Crowell), 2010.
- "Skipping decisions must meet additional statutory requirements" beyond mere
  competency — Templeton (Reyes), 2010.

**The stable losing move — "we retained the better candidate."**
- "The Education Code does not permit districts in a layoff proceeding to weigh
  the qualifications of teaching staff and retain junior employees it believes
  have superior skills." — Stockton (Sark), 2010; echoed verbatim Oroville Union
  High (Westmore), 2012.

## What actually succeeds vs. fails (structured 2009 holdings)

**District wins — clean record of a specific need + a senior evidentiary gap:**
- Junior had special training for an online independent-study program the senior
  lacked — Ramona Unified (Johnson), 2009 (district).
- Junior had specific English-Learner-Literacy-coach training the senior
  respondents "failed to demonstrate they possessed" — Lancaster (Montoya), 2009
  (district).
- A "minimally sufficient showing" of special fitness sufficed where respondents
  "provided no evidence" senior employees had the training — Sacramento City
  (Frink), 2009 (district). *(The bar is real but not high when uncontested.)*

**District losses — the failure migrated to drafting and proving the negative:**
- District proved the junior's special skills for only the **.2 ROP fraction** of
  the assignment and failed to prove the senior lacked training for the rest →
  improper skip — Acalanes (Anderson), 2008 (respondent).
- District invoked **NCLB/HOUSSE compliance** as the competency criterion, but the
  **PKS resolution never defined competency to include it** and the district did
  not show it was necessary for the assignment → failed the (d)(1) showing,
  notice rescinded — Sylvan Union (Brandt), 2009 (respondent).

## Practitioner takeaway (and where it feeds)

The exposure is **not** a moving doctrinal target — it is (i) **resolution
drafting** (name the skip/competency criterion in the Board's PKS resolution and
tie it to a specific program need) and (ii) **proof of the negative**
(affirmatively prove senior teachers lack the *necessary* training, not merely
that the junior has it). This is precisely the surface a **P3 resolution linter**
would check, and the kind of issue-specific risk a **P1 matter memo** would lead
with.

## Honesty caveats

- Structured outcome detail (arguments + who-won) exists only for 2004/2009;
  the win/lose examples above are from 2009.
- This trace sampled the **endpoints** (1981–83 and 2008–12) more than the
  1990s–2000s middle; the "formalization over time" claim is well-supported, and
  the "substantive bar unchanged" claim rests on the consistent *Alexander* /
  *Poppers* anchoring, but would be firmer with denser middle-era sampling. The
  corpus *has* those years — this was a scoping choice, not a data gap.
